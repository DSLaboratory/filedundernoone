The pitch

The book stops at the fence. The game is the night it won't narrate.

For eleven years, Margaret Vane sat at a government records desk and decided what the country was allowed to see — and what could go unwatched. She drew the blind spots herself. Then she reported a ledger she was never meant to read, and by morning she had been struck from every register that ever held her name. Filed Under No One is two halves of one story: a novel that walks her to the fence and stops — and a top-down stealth game that is the night she goes back in, crossing the building she used to run to recover the only proof she ever existed.

How it plays

Pure stealth. No combat, no weapons, no health bar.

Light is the whole game

Guards see in hard wedges of light that sweep the floor, line-of-sight checked tile by tile. Corners block sight. To win, you simply are never seen.

The shadow rule

Step onto a shadow tile and a guard staring straight at you cannot see you. Binary, legible, ruthless.

One detection meter

It fills in under a second of unbroken sight and drains the instant you break it. Fill it and you're taken — no second chances.

Sound vs sight

Sound only ever sends a guard to look; only being seen raises the alarm. Run and you're fast — and loud. Sneak and you leave nothing.

Tools of the trade

Lure a patrol with a deliberate knock. Read a corner radar of guard facings — until you go loud and it dissolves into static.

Controls

Arrows / WASD, run on Shift, action on Space / Z. Full keyboard rebinding; gamepad supported.

Calm

The patrol keeps its pattern. You are a rumour the building doesn't believe.

Suspicious

A cone clipped you; a head turns. One held breath to stop being there.

Hunting

Seen in full light. The floor goes loud — and it knows the dark too.

World & characters

Margaret Vane

Twenty-seven; eleven years at the records desk. She maps the blind spots of others for a living and never once turned the lamp on her own. Her face is never shown in the trailer, the art, or this kit — the work sells the question of who she is now, not a portrait.

The Director

The one figure who never leaves his post, who still watches his own door in the dark at the end of the last night — the only man who would know her if he turned around. The game never shows you whether he does.

A legacy paper basement under a present-day building of screens and keycards. Institutional spy fiction in the le Carré tradition, where the most dangerous object in the room is a rubber stamp.

Sound

Entirely procedural — even the music.

No audio files — every sound is synthesised live in code. The main menu has its own music, a cinematic drone-bed; then the floor itself plays in silence, where sound marks change: papery footsteps, a rising "?" chirp and a two-voice "!" stab panned toward the guard who made them, and a heartbeat tempo-locked to the detection meter — 60 to 180 BPM as capture nears, silent the instant you're safe.

Look

Drawn in code, in the playfield.

No textures, no sprite sheets, no loaded images in the playfield — every wall, cone, shadow, and character is rendered live from Canvas 2D primitives, the player and guards hand-pixelled pixel by pixel. The austerity is the point: dark grids, pale cones, true-black shadow, and one rationed amber signal — of a piece with the novel's cover. The form is the theme.

What makes it different

One story, two media, told without contradiction.

The novel and the game are a single artefact — the game is the climax the book deliberately won't narrate; finish the night, read where it came from, in the same browser. A stealth game with no combat, its whole playfield drawn in code, whose form is its theme: surveillance and erasure rendered as light, shadow, and a held breath. Built on a hand-written, fully deterministic engine with zero runtime dependencies — every run a tape you can replay exactly. And you play the system's victim, slipping through the very blind spots she designed.

Who made it

DS Laboratory is a one-person studio in London. Filed Under No One — its engine, its prose, its levels — was designed, written, and directed to one standard by one person, David Silva, with AI carrying out much of the execution under that direction. The studio states this plainly: the decisions, the testing, and the bar are one author's.
